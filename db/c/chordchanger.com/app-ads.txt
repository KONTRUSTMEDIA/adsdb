        <h2>How to Transpose Guitar Chords</h2>
        <p>Transposing chords means shifting all the chords in a song up or down by the same interval. This is useful when a song's original key doesn't suit your vocal range, or when you want to play along with other musicians in a different key.</p>

        <h3>When to Transpose</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Vocal range:</strong> If a song is too high or low for your voice, transpose it to a comfortable key.</li>
          <li><strong>Easier chords:</strong> Some keys use simpler chord shapes. Transposing from F to G eliminates barre chords.</li>
          <li><strong>Playing with others:</strong> Match keys with other instruments or singers.</li>
          <li><strong>Capo alternatives:</strong> Instead of using a capo, transpose the written chords down to play open positions.</li>
        </ul>

        <h3>Transposing vs. Using a Capo</h3>
        <p>A capo raises the pitch of your guitar without changing the chord shapes you play. Transposing changes the actual chord names and fingerings. Both achieve the same result—playing in a different key—but transposing gives you more flexibility.</p>

        <h3>How Chordchanger Works</h3>
        <p>Paste any chord sheet into the editor, then use the transpose buttons to shift all chords up or down by semitones. The tool automatically recognizes chord names and transposes them while preserving lyrics and formatting. Chord diagrams update instantly to show fingerings for each transposed chord. Set a capo position to see adjusted shapes, use auto-scroll for hands-free playing, and save songs to your local library.</p>

        <h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

        <h4>Do I need to know music theory to transpose chords?</h4>
        <p>No! Chordchanger handles all the music theory for you. Simply enter your song text and click the transpose buttons.</p>

        <h4>What does "transpose up 2 steps" mean?</h4>
        <p>Each step (semitone) is the distance from one fret to the next. Transposing up 2 steps moves every chord up by 2 semitones. For example, C becomes D, G becomes A, and Am becomes Bm.</p>

        <h4>Is using a capo the same as transposing?</h4>
        <p>They achieve the same musical result but work differently. A capo physically shortens your strings while you play the same shapes. Transposing changes which chord shapes you play. Chordchanger supports both with its built-in capo selector.</p>

        <h4>Can I transpose ukulele chords too?</h4>
        <p>Yes! Switch to ukulele mode and Chordchanger will show ukulele chord diagrams instead of guitar diagrams.</p>
