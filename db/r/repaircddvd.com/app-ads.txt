Structure of Media, A standard compact disc (also known as an audio CD) stores digital audio files on the disc in accordance with a "Red Book" standard distinguishing them from later versions. These disks are composed of a 1.2 mm thick layer of polycarbonate with a diameter of 12 cm covered with a thin aluminum layer encoded with information as micro-grooves and flat areas, or lands. They are read by a semiconductor laser (AlGaAs) that generates light at a frequency of approximately 780 nm wavelength. The information is stored in a spiral pattern extending from the center of the disk to the outer edge. The rotational speed changes so that the linear velocity of the read head relative to the track is constant; at reading speed x1, this is between 1.2 ms and 1.4 ms. Reading occurs from the inside out while the rotational speed decreases as distance from the center of the disk increases. Prior to disk writing, information is encoded according to an "8 in 14" standard that is stored on the disk as lands and pits in embossed plates; these recesses are approximately one-quarter wavelength deep relative to the material being read by the laser, or about 125 nm. Interference from reflected light generates variable patterns depending on these grooves-or pits-approximating 500 nm wide while the track spacing has been set at 1.6 mm.
