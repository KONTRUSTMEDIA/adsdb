<li role="listitem"><strong>A father from Telford has said inclusive ski sessions have transformed the life of his daughter, who has special educational needs.</strong></li>

<div data-block="text">
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<p>Emma Buckley-Robins, 22, attends sessions run by staff at the Telford Snowboarding and Ski Centre in Madeley.</p>
<p>Her father Simon said she had the cognitive ability of a "two to three-year-old", but that the ski sessions had given her more confidence.</p>
<p>"Before, she couldn't do anything. We had to strap her into one of the little buggies and they [ski instructors] would take her down the slope," he said.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>"She's progressed from that to being able to go up the ski lift to the top by herself, which is just absolutely amazing.</p>
<p>"We were in tears when we first saw it because we never thought she would ever do that, to ski down halfway by herself."</p>

<p>Ski instructors at the centre help children and young adults with a wide range of disabilities and needs.</p>
<p>Staff member Jordan Gripton said: "It's an activity for them to do, so it gives them that social basis so they're not having nothing to do.</p>
<p>"They have got an activity they can enjoy it and it's something they'll keep coming back to.</p>
<p>"You can tell when they are nervous but then obviously when they get used to it, it disappears slowly."</p>
<p>Simon Buckley-Robins, who cares for his daughter with his wife Mandy, said the family's experience showed what children can do "with the right support and the right places to go to".</p>
<p>"We as families really try our hardest because our children deserve the same as every other child. They just need to be taught differently," he said.</p>
<p>"As much as we keep pushing, unless you have the right staffing out there to be able to put these sessions on, it just literally can't happen."</p>

<div data-testid="rich-text">
<p>Dominic Hall received 2.5 million views on a TikTok video showcasing how he plays the game Mortal Kombat by using audio cues.</p>
<p>Hall would be conducting a fundraising live stream for iSightCornwall, a sight loss charity based in Cornwall, on Twitch on 13 February from 17:00 GMT to 19:00.</p>
<p>Hall, who worked as a volunteer for the charity and then as a member of staff, said it filled him with pride when he saw someone leave an appointment being able to do something they thought was impossible after losing their sight.</p>

<p>When Hall was three years old he was diagnosed with a brain tumour in the central cluster at the front of his brain.</p>
<p>iSightCornwall said this completely damaged the optic nerve in his right eye and damaged the left eye, leaving him with 10% vision.</p>
<p>Hall learned to play video games by adapting set ups, sitting close to the TV, having magnifiers attached to handheld consoles and experimenting with game genres.</p>
<p>It added in 2022 he lost the remaining sight in his left eye but that didn't stop him from continuing to game.</p>

<div>
<p>The Sign Language Bill (Northern Ireland) 2025 was introduced in the assembly by Communities Minister Gordon Lyons in February 2025</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>The bill would give official and equal recognition of British Sign Language (BSL) and Irish Sign Language (ISL) as languages of Northern Ireland and promote the use and understanding of sign languages.</p>
<p>It means that public bodies will have to, by law, "take reasonable steps to ensure that the sorts of information and services provided by it are as accessible to individuals in the deaf community as to those individuals who are not in the deaf community".</p>
<p>The Department for Communities estimated that at least 5000 people in Northern Ireland use either British Sign Language (BSL) or Irish Sign Language (ISL) as their preferred way to communicate.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="subheadline">
<div>
<div data-testid="subheadline">
<h2 id="Very-tough" tabindex="-1"><span role="text">'Very tough'</span></h2>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>Ellie-May is one of only three deaf pupils at her post-primary school.</p>
<p>"It is very tough because you're deaf and you're surrounded by hearing people at a mainstream school, and you have a lot to concentrate on," she said.</p>
<p>"When you're lip reading you're absorbing information and your brain is processing differently from a hearing person."</p>
<p>Ellie-May would like to see more young people in school learn to sign.</p>
<p>"Sign language is very good because obviously you can communicate to people better and you can just sign," she said.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="image">
<div>

Gemma McMullan's two-year-old son George is deaf</div>
<p><br />Gemma McMullan, whose two-year old son George is deaf, said deaf children will "be recognised as part of the community" due to the new laws.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<p>George was first identified as deaf at only eight weeks old and has been attending sign language classes at Action Deaf Youth.</p>
<p>"Sign language isn't just a communication tool, it's going to be recognised as an official language and that's really important," she said.</p>
<p>"Our deaf children are going to be seen and they're going to be recognised as part of the community and they're going to have more access to education, healthcare, things like that so it's really important that our children are recognised and using their own language as part of their identity."</p>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>Julie Graham from Action Deaf Youth, an organisation for deaf children and young people, said it is a "massive, a massive movement forward".</p>
<p>"What's crucial is particularly to look at deaf children and young people because this will transform their futures."</p>

<p>The British Deaf Association's Majella McAteer told BBC News NI that the finger spelling differs between the two sign languages.</p>
<p>"We spell out words with two hands in British Sign Language and with one hand in Irish Sign Language, but our numbers as well in BSL use one hand, but our numbers in ISL use two hands. So there are big differences," she said.</p>
<p>"Here in Northern Ireland it's something that's very unique to have two sign languages that are used and the majority of our deaf community will know both languages.</p>
<p>"Some will use British sign language, some will predominantly use ISL, it depends where you were educated."</p>

<p>"In practical terms it's going to mean that all public bodies will have to provide services to people in their first languages," he said.</p>
<p>"It gives official recognition to both BSL and ISL and it recognises that those languages are the first language of people when they're using services."</p>

<p>The expectation is that the bill will be passed into law by MLAs before the next Assembly elections in May 2027.</p>

<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p><strong>Merlin Entertainments has backtracked on its controversial plan to tighten access to its disability queuing system at its theme parks following strong criticism from people with autism, ADHD and anxiety.</strong></p>
<p>The company had planned to introduce new eligibility rules for its Ride Access Pass (RAP), which provides a non-queueing option for people unable to stand in long lines.</p>
<p>Under the plan, visitors to places like Alton Towers, Chessington World of Adventures and Legoland Windsor this half-term who only had "difficulty with crowds" would no longer have qualified.</p>
<p>Merlin said it had "listened carefully to the extensive feedback" and decided to "pause the trial while we explore alternative options".</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="subheadline">
<div>
<div data-testid="subheadline">
<h2 id="Theyve-told-us-loud-and-clear" tabindex="-1"><span role="text">'They've told us loud and clear'</span></h2>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>The theme park owner addressed those people "impacted" by their previous announcement, saying: "We are sorry."</p>
<p>The move sparked widespread upset among adults with autism, ADHD and anxiety and families of neurodivergent children - who said the changes risked shutting many people out of a system they rely on.</p>
<p>More than 25,000 people signed an online petition urging the company to reverse the plan.</p>
<p>Merlin announced on Thursday that it will not go ahead with the trial following "extensive feedback".</p>
<p>Rob Smith, the company's chief operating officer, said the company had heard from many RAP users who felt the proposed changes would worsen accessibility rather than improve it.</p>
<p>"Whenever we make changes to the Ride Access Pass, it's because we genuinely want to improve the experience for guests who rely on it," he said.</p>
<p>"They've told us loud and clear that the current system isn't working."</p>
<p>Smith said the need to change the disability queuing system remained because the Ride Access Pass was "under huge pressure", but that the company believed it was "important to take the time to fully reflect" on the feedback.</p>
<p>He added: "For those who were impacted by our recent announcement, we are sorry."</p>
<p>With more and more people claiming the pass, some disabled visitors were facing longer waits than the main queue, which the company said was "not the experience we want for anyone".</p>
<p>Merlin said it will now launch a broader consultation with disabled guests, accessibility specialists and other operators across the attractions industry.</p>
</div>

<div data-testid="subheadline">
<h2 id="What-sparked-the-backlash" tabindex="-1"><span role="text">What sparked the backlash?</span></h2>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>Last week,&nbsp;<a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cx2gwgj4xreo">BBC News revealed</a>&nbsp;that Alton Towers planned to stop automatically granting its disability queuing pass to some guests with autism, ADHD, anxiety and other non-physical disabilities, if their Nimbus access pass only indicated they struggled being in crowds.</p>
<p>Many families contacted BBC Your Voice to express their frustration and to say that it would stop them from taking their neurodivergent children to the Merlin theme parks they loved.</p>
<p>Some described the proposed system as "unworkable", while charities warned it risked creating new barriers in an already difficult environment.</p>
<p>The now-aborted plan followed an update by the disability access card provider Nimbus Disability which Merlin uses to verify additional needs.</p>
<p>Nimbus has split its former "standing and queuing" card section into two separate symbols: "difficulty standing" (a physical need) and "difficulty with crowds" (often linked to neurodivergence or anxiety).</p>
<p>Nimbus Disability Managing director Martin Austin said: "We developed the Access Card in response to frustrations shared by disabled people and venues about how disability was evidenced.</p>
<p>"Merlin has been considering how these criteria relate to its Ride Access Pass... Accessibility is constantly evolving and improving, and we welcome Merlin's willingness to listen to feedback."</p>
<p>Nimbus said it remained in "close dialogue" with Merlin and the wider industry about how its system might best support both disabled guests and operators.</p>
<p>The British Association of Leisure Parks, Piers &amp; Attractions (BALPPA) said that pressure on accessibility systems went beyond the parks owned by Merlin.</p>
<p>"British attractions have made significant progress with accessibility, but are facing operational challenges with increasing demand from guests," BALPPA chief executive Paul Kelly said.</p>
<p>"We support Nimbus's continued efforts... Their input will be invaluable as we continue to evolve our approach."</p>
</div>

<h2 id="What-happens-now" tabindex="-1"><span role="text">What happens now?</span></h2>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div data-block="text">
<div>
<div data-testid="rich-text">
<div>
<p>Merlin will continue using its existing RAP criteria, including acceptance of the Access Card "crowd" symbol as it currently operates.</p>
<p>A video message from Smith has been issued to visitors explaining the decision.</p>
