          React Native CLI vs Native – Real Story from My Recent Delivered Project

Back in September 2024, I worked on a high-traffic app with millions of users across the Middle Gulf countries. The app was originally built in native iOS and Android, but later I rebuilt it completely from scratch using React Native CLI.

Performance? React Native CLI Outperformed the Native Builds

Yes, you heard that right, React Native CLI outperformed the previous native builds. When it comes to app performance, I strongly believe it mostly depends on the developer, not just the tech stack. While selecting the right technology for the use case is important, how it's implemented makes all the difference.

My Approach (Overview):
- Spent 4–5 days setting up a simple and modular architecture
- Identified and reused components wherever possible
- Checked every 3rd-party SDK before using
- Didn’t use any extra or deprecated packages — built custom ones if needed
- Used FlashList instead of FlatList for better list performance
- Tracked which components re-render and fixed the root issues
- Memoized components, functions, and values properly
- Worked closely with backend — removed extra data from APIs
- Never over-engineered — wrote code that anyone can read easily
- Used try-catch not just for APIs, but around user interactions too

- Always thought about time complexity while writing any logic (e.g., O(n) is fine, but avoid O(n²) when possible). For example: many devs update an array by looping with .map() to match item IDs (O(n)), but if the index is already known, it's better to update the array directly using array[index] = newItem — which is O(1) and much faster.

- And honestly — I did a lot more, too much to write in one short post

Final Results:
- Android APK size was just 11 MB, iOS build 50 MB
- Crash-free rate: 99.99%
- App opening time was much faster than the native one
- Better UI/UX experience compared to the older build

One thing I learned again:
 It’s not just about the tech, it’s how you write and handle the code.

Lastly, I strongly recommend the React Native Optimization Guide by Callstack, it gave me some really insightful knowledge and helped shape the way I approached performance in this project.

          Learning 5 from talabat: Tech Debt is not an Engineering problem, it's an Everyone problem.

The benefit of being in a business that has to deliver millions of orders a day is that if you have a flaky system, you will feel it pretty quickly. At talabat, we leaned on 21 years of legacy, while scaling 30-40% year-on-year across multiple verticals. That kind of scale pushes the boundaries of every system - downtime kicks in, rework spikes, and if not managed carefully, it can really affect the Tech &amp; Product relationship.

The thing about being in an ops-heavy business also meant any downtime meant zero revenues. It was at talabat that I felt that not investing in solving for our tech debt would really come and bite me as a Product person. 

1) Stacking Tech Debt up against Other work
What I really liked about how we approached tech-debt topics is that we treated it as a product - we discussed and debated how investing in it would reduce cost, improve cycle time, and help improve time-to-market for what we delivered to our customers. As imprecise as business impact numbers were on these topics, I was glad that our Engineering leadership really attempted to stack things up against the more measurable Commercial bets that we have in the company.

Fundamentally though, this is where the CPO-CTO relationship trust and relationship (See Lesson 3) is extremely important. This is where you use your "trust credits" and trust that your tech counterpart is doing right by your customers and business and back them all the way.

2) Case Study: Moving to Flutter
As with every product, we also try not and "big-bang" change (to the best extent). As an example, Our CTO, Sven, advocated very early in 2020 that we should be investing in cross-platform mobile development capabilities. We wanted to streamline mobile development - dropping the overhead of managing feature gaps between iOS and Android, and optimizing resource allocation. It wasn't until 2024 that we were fully done with moving every-single-page onto Flutter. Slow and steady does win the race here. 

Looking back, this decision unlocked simplicity, which unlocked a ton of speed - we can now iterate faster, test more things, and deliver more value to our business and customers. That's some pretty good ROI I'd say!

          For everyone in Lebanon asking me about freelancing

Here’s a quick brief from my experience — with a promise to share more detailed insights once I have time for content.

My start
I self-learned programming, applied to local jobs on LinkedIn, got interviews, passed tests… but at the last step? Ghosted every time.
No idea why. Maybe too old for a junior role. Maybe my lack of skills then, other reasons.

So I went all-in on Upwork: studied tips, applied daily, and landed my first client in 3 days.
Some people take 1–3 months — that’s fine. It works.
I used the same approach for my brother; he got his first client in 2 months.

After the first client
It gets easier — but harder in new ways. Early on: low income, lots of hustle. Expect 6–12 months of grind before stability.

Myths
• “Freelancers work whenever they want.”
Reality: To stay safe, you usually work slightly more than full-time. Keep one extra client above your comfort zone, because even “long-term” ones (months or years) can vanish.

• “Freelancing is just small gigs.”
Reality: No. It’s real product work in teams (designers, devs, PMs) — same as employment, just different contract terms.

Contract types
• Hourly — track time and get paid for every minute. 12h = 12h paid. 3h = 3h paid.
• Milestone — fixed package of tasks, paid once delivered.
• Weekly retainer — like booking your time. Example: 15h/week at $10/h = $150/week guaranteed, whether they give you full tasks or not. Extra hours billed on top.

Why I prefer it over employment
• Diversify risk: instead of 40h for one company, split across 2–3 clients.
• More experience, tools, networks.
• Multiple income streams + stronger portfolio.
• Remote flexibility.
• Fewer interview loops. Once proven, clients come to you.

The catch
• No paid vacations/sick days.
• You must deliver the hours you commit.
• Strong time/energy management.
• Always in push mode — I worked Sat/Sun for 2 years; only recently I take Sundays off.

But if you’re sick or need a day, you can shift your schedule. That flexibility is real.

One more myth
That “laptop lifestyle” — $100K from the beach, 2h/day — is marketing to sell courses.
That’s not freelancing. It only appears when you build a business: a team, delegation, and stepping away from daily work.

Freelancing is grind: coding, design, client calls. The upside: you control clients and schedule. I’m building toward the business side with my agency, but freelancing itself isn’t a shortcut.

Employment or freelancing First?
No single answer.
Some say: get a job first, then freelance.
Others (like me): start freelancing from day one — yes, even as a junior.

How to start
• Learn everything you can about freelancing &amp; Upwork.
• Apply consistently.
• No views → fix proposals.
• No interviews → fix profile/portfolio.
• Interviews but no offers → fix sales pitch.
• Sometimes it’s luck or competition — focus on what you control.

          This team is building B2C fintech in Iraq. 

It's processed around $12M in total transaction value.

There's some granularity to understand here. 

Simma and its founder (Samer I. Tarazi) understood that building fintech in Iraq wasn't actually a tech problem. It was a trust problem. Iraqis had trust issues with bestowing their money to banks and preferred keeping their money safely in cash, under a mattress, at home. 

Samer reasoned that building fintech in Iraq meant, before anything, getting Iraqi consumers to trust depositing their cash in exchange for a digital equivalent. 

As a result, here's the tiered-approach Simma went through, as explained by Samer:

"We designed three phases.

The first phase was acquiring and understanding users. We launched a mobile app, which acted as an e-commerce site replete with foreign brands. We productized our MVP, essentially. We were still buying for users manually on the back-end. For the first 12 months, all orders were paid via cash-on-delivery (COD). That phase was necessary: if we entered the market from day 1 with a digital payment solution, no one would’ve trusted us and we would’ve flopped. 

This positioning is powerful in another way: we’re not coming in as a “financial institution”, but rather as a useful, reliable purveyor of digital services that happens to offer more and more digital services, including financial ones. 

The second phase was moving people away from cash and introducing digital payment. 

We built a Simma wallet, which users could top up by buying a physical voucher online. They’d have it physically delivered, pay the delivery driver in cash, and the wallet was topped up once the driver collected the cash. The voucher system fits into one of my theses about Iraqi society: if people give you cash, they expect something physical in return. The voucher plays that role. Today, around 25% of Simma’s transactions are done via the wallet.

The third phase, which we are about to enter, is the introduction of digital financial services, such as a buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) option. These new financial services won’t be launched in the void. They have to serve Simma’s value proposition, which is helping Iraqis buy stuff from abroad. If the new financial product doesn’t power that value proposition, users have no incentive to adopt it."

Going back to that initial $12M transaction value: most of it is actually cash-on-delivery, but Simma is set on growing the share of it happening digitally. Step by step.

Samer reasoned that building fintech in Iraq meant, before anything, getting Iraqi consumers to trust depositing their cash in exchange for a digital equivalent. 

As a result, here's the tiered-approach Simma went through, as explained by Samer:

&quot;We designed three phases.

The first phase was acquiring and understanding users. We launched a mobile app, which acted as an e-commerce site replete with foreign brands. We productized our MVP, essentially. We were still buying for users manually on the back-end. For the first 12 months, all orders were paid via cash-on-delivery (COD). That phase was necessary: if we entered the market from day 1 with a digital payment solution, no one would’ve trusted us and we would’ve flopped. 

This positioning is powerful in another way: we’re not coming in as a “financial institution”, but rather as a useful, reliable purveyor of digital services that happens to offer more and more digital services, including financial ones. 

The second phase was moving people away from cash and introducing digital payment. 

We built a Simma wallet, which users could top up by buying a physical voucher online. They’d have it physically delivered, pay the delivery driver in cash, and the wallet was topped up once the driver collected the cash. The voucher system fits into one of my theses about Iraqi society: if people give you cash, they expect something physical in return. The voucher plays that role. Today, around 25% of Simma’s transactions are done via the wallet.

The third phase, which we are about to enter, is the introduction of digital financial services, such as a buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) option. These new financial services won’t be launched in the void. They have to serve Simma’s value proposition, which is helping Iraqis buy stuff from abroad. If the new financial product doesn’t power that value proposition, users have no incentive to adopt it.&quot;

Going back to that initial $12M transaction value: most of it is actually cash-on-delivery, but Simma is set on growing the share of it happening digitally. Step by step.

Egypt now has 46.3 million active mobile wallets. Financial inclusion reached 76.3%, up from 27% in 2021. Transaction volumes hit EGP 943 billion in a single quarter. By any measure, this is one of the fastest inclusion stories in the developing world.

But inclusion and transformation are not the same thing.

Here is how Egyptians actually use their wallets, per the NTRA:

Wallet-to-wallet transfers: 54% of transactions, 71% of total value. 
Mobile and internet top-ups: 20%. 
Deposits: 19%. 
Withdrawals: 5% of transactions, 11% of value.
54 + 20 + 19 + 5 = 98%

When money does leave wallets as cash disbursements, 79% exits as physical cash withdrawal. Only 15% goes to payments like bills or shopping.

And on the inflow side, 65% of deposits arrive via InstaPay bank transfers. Money enters wallets digitally because it's convenient. It exits as cash because the ecosystem hasn't given people a reason to keep it digital.

Egypt didn't build a digital payments economy.

It built the most sophisticated cash distribution network in Africa.

76.3% is a genuine achievement. But having an account is not the same as changing behavior.

The next phase of Egyptian fintech is not about opening more wallets.

It's about building something worth spending from.

          I have conducted 100s of interviews. Here’s what I learned:
People don’t join startups. They join founders.

When I joined Careem (a young Middle Eastern ride-hailing company at the time), it was not because of the title or the salary package.
It was because of the demeanor and conviction of Careem’s MD Junaid Iqbal during the interview.

I remember thinking… this is the man I want to learn from. The vision I want to bet on.

That decision shaped my career and taught me something I have carried ever since:
Recruitment is branding.

Not branding in the marketing sense.
Branding in the sense that when a candidate sits across the table, what they are really evaluating is you.

• Your vision
• Your passion
• Your values
• Your credibility

If those shine through, A-players will join you even when resources are scarce.
If they do not, no amount of salary or perks will save you.

I have personally witnessed this time and time again.

The best hires I have ever made did not sign up for the job description.
They signed up for me.
For my belief that we could win.

So if you are a founder struggling to attract talent, don’t just polish your JD.
Polish your story.

Because in the end, people don’t join logos.
They join leaders.
